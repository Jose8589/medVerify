Why you couldn't find this yourself

The information exists. It just wasn't built to be found by you.

In the U.S. your surgeon was credentialed by a hospital and everything is searchable in seconds. In Mexico, all of it exists too — but scattered, in Spanish, and indexed for no one.

In the United States

  • License searchable in a public state database
  • Board certification verified in 30 seconds
  • Complaint history centralized and public
  • Hospital credentialing did the vetting for you

In Mexico

  • Professional license lives in the SEP registry
  • Specialty license — a different part of the same system
  • Certification validity in CONACEM — only by exact specialty code
  • Sanctions in COFEPRIS — not indexed by Google at all

It's not that you didn't search well enough. It's that this information was built for regulators, not for patients — and reading it correctly takes knowing exactly where, and how, to look.

Three patterns we document regularly

None of these would show up in a Google search.

01

The certification no one renewed

Specialty certification in Mexico expires. Many doctors keep publishing the credentials they had eight years ago — because nobody requires them to update it, and nobody checks. Until someone verifies.

02

The clinic that changed its name

A clinic with COFEPRIS sanctions can close and reopen under a new name at the same address. Reviews start at zero; old alerts don't follow. From the outside it looks brand new and spotless.

03

The doctor who isn't the doctor

Doctors can share nearly identical names. The surgeon with 300 reviews and the one with three COFEPRIS alerts may have the same name. Search the wrong registry and you verify the wrong person entirely.

Frequently asked questions

If you searched for this, you're in the right place.

In Mexico, board certification is issued by CONACEM (Consejo Nacional de Certificación de Especialidades Médicas). Unlike in the U.S., it expires — typically every five years — and must be renewed. A doctor can have a valid SEP specialty license and still have an expired CONACEM certification, meaning their specialty credentials haven't been reviewed in years. We verify both directly in each registry.

COFEPRIS is Mexico's federal health regulatory authority — roughly the FDA's enforcement role. They issue alerts, sanctions, and can order clinic closures. We report what type of alert exists, when it was issued, and what it referred to — so you can judge the severity yourself.

A cédula profesional confirms a completed medical degree and authorization to practice. A cédula de especialidad confirms a recognized specialty residency. A doctor can have the first without the second — a licensed physician, but not a credentialed specialist in your procedure. We verify both independently.

It depends almost entirely on how far your clinic is from a hospital with an ICU — sometimes 5 minutes, sometimes 40. Your report includes the specific nearest hospital, the estimated transfer time, and the nearest U.S. or Canadian consulate, which can provide a list of local physicians, help with emergency communication, and assist with repatriation.

That depends entirely on the specific doctor and clinic — which is exactly why verification exists. Mexico has JCI-accredited hospitals and surgeons trained at leading U.S. institutions. It also has unlicensed practitioners and clinics without current permits. The country isn't the variable. The specific provider is.

All the registries are in Spanish — CONACEM, the SEP registry, and the COFEPRIS alert system have no English version. That's the core reason this service exists. We navigate those systems, interpret what we find, and deliver the report in plain English.

SEP licenses carry a unique registration number verifiable in the federal registry, so the credential itself can't be faked — but someone can present a real credential belonging to a similarly named person, or present a legitimate one while omitting that their specialty certification has expired. Our homonym check and cross-verification are designed to catch exactly that.
